# Gems of Light
### A Vision Document & Project Brief

---

## The One-Line Pitch

A gentle side-scrolling platformer where children collect glowing gems — each one an ayah — and arrange them into a complete surah, learning the Qur'an through play, repetition, and wonder.

---

## Why This Exists

Qur'anic memorization is built on repetition. A child hears an ayah, repeats it, hears it again, repeats it again, until the words settle into them. This is beautiful and it is also, for a six-year-old, often boring.

The insight behind this game is simple: **repetition is also the backbone of play.** Children will run the same level thirty times. They will replay a favorite moment for the pleasure of it. If the thing being repeated is an ayah — heard clearly, in order, in a moment of delight — the memorization happens sideways, without ever feeling like study.

We are not gamifying the Qur'an. We are giving a child a reason to hear it again.

---

## Who This Is For

**The player: ages 5–8.**
A child with limited finger dexterity and enormous curiosity. They can't read fluently. They can't manage a menu system. They lose interest when they lose. They light up when something sparkles and makes a sound. They are learning to sequence, to sort, to hold a pattern in their head.

They may be memorizing surahs already — at home, at weekend school, with a parent at bedtime. This game is a companion to that, not a replacement for it. It is what they do in the twenty minutes before dinner.

**The gatekeeper: the parent.**
A parent who wants their child's screen time to be worth something. Who is wary of anything that treats the Qur'an casually or cheaply. Who will not install this if the recitation sounds wrong, if the art feels tacky, if the whole thing smells of a worksheet in a costume.

The parent must feel, on first glance, that this was made with care by someone who understands what is being handled.

---

## The Feeling We Are Chasing

Quiet, warm, unhurried.

Not a game of noise and flashing. Not a game with a countdown clock or a losing screen. There is no urgency here. A child should be able to stand still in a level, doing nothing at all, listening to the wind and the birds, and never be punished for it.

The closest reference is not Mario, despite the shape of the thing. It's closer to *Journey*, *Gris*, or a Studio Ghibli field. The pleasure is in the moving, the finding, the light on the water. Mario gives us the grammar. The mood comes from somewhere gentler.

---

## Visual Aesthetic

**Palette.** Green is the spine of the game. Not one green — dozens. Mint, sage, moss, olive, pine, jade, seafoam, eucalyptus. The world is rendered in these greens, layered against warm sand, cream stone, and a sky that shifts from morning gold to evening rose depending on the world. Gold is used sparingly and only for moments of reverence — the glow of a gem, the light at the end of a level.

**Style.** Soft, hand-painted, slightly textured — like gouache on thick paper. Nothing is glossy. Nothing has a hard black outline. Edges are a little soft, colors bleed gently into each other. Backgrounds are painterly and impressionistic; foreground platforms are crisp enough to read clearly as surfaces you can stand on.

**Ornament, not iconography.** The visual language draws from Islamic geometric pattern and vegetal arabesque — eight-pointed stars, interlacing tessellation, vines that curl across the stone. These appear in architecture, in tilework on platforms, in the borders of the interface. They are decorative and structural, never representational. **No figurative depictions of prophets, angels, or sacred events. No calligraphy used as a platform, a floor, or anything to be stood upon or jumped over.** The written word, where it appears, appears with dignity — framed, still, never underfoot.

**The player character.** A small child in simple clothes, or better, an abstracted figure — a small round creature made of light, perhaps, or a bird. Non-gendered, non-specific, easy for any child to inhabit. It moves with a soft bounce. When it lands, dust puffs. When it stops, it looks up.

**The gems.** Each is a faceted stone of a distinct green, glowing faintly from within, turning slowly in the air. They are the brightest thing on screen. A child's eye finds them instantly.

---

## Gameplay

**Movement.** Walk left, walk right, jump. That is the entire vocabulary. No running, no sprinting, no double-jump, no crouch. The character moves at a stroll. Jumps are floaty and long-hanging, generous at the apex, forgiving on landing — a jump that looks like it should miss will usually catch the ledge.

**No failure.** There are no lives. There is no death. Falling into water lifts you gently back to the bank. Touching a creature does not hurt you — creatures here are not enemies, they are inhabitants, and they wander past you or hop out of your way. The child cannot lose. They can only take longer.

This is not a concession to age. It is a design position. Nothing about this experience should carry the emotional charge of failing at the Qur'an.

**The collecting.** Each level is one surah. Scattered through the level are gems, one for each ayah. They are placed to reward exploration — behind a waterfall, atop a high arch, tucked into an alcove — but never so hidden that a patient child cannot find them. A soft chime and a warm pulse of light signals a gem is nearby.

When a gem is touched, the level goes quiet. The ayah is recited — clearly, unhurried, in a single beautiful voice. The gem's light travels up into a small band at the top of the screen and settles there. Then the world's ambient sound returns and the child moves on.

**Crucially: the gems can be collected in any order.** The child wanders and finds them as they find them. The ordering is not the challenge of the level. The ordering is the gate at the end.

**The gate.** At the level's close, the child arrives at a still place — a courtyard, a pool, a small arch of carved stone. The collected gems float in a loose cloud. Below them, a row of empty settings in the stone.

The child arranges the gems in the order of the ayahs. Tapping a gem replays its recitation. Placing a gem in a setting locks it with a soft click of light. When the sequence is complete and correct, the settings ignite one by one and **the full surah is recited from beginning to end**, each gem glowing in turn as its ayah is spoken. The arch opens. The child walks through.

**When the child is wrong,** the gem simply drifts back out of the setting. Nothing scolds. Nothing buzzes. After a few attempts, one gem begins to glow — a hint. After a few more, the correct order fills itself in and the door opens anyway. **Nobody is ever locked out of a surah.**

## Progression

The game is organized around the natural progression of memorization: short surahs first, from the end of the Qur'an, moving forward.

### World One — The Garden
*Al-Fatiha, Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, An-Nas, Al-Kawthar, Al-Asr*

Morning light, wet grass, low stone walls. The gentlest possible introduction. Levels are short — two or three minutes. Surahs have three to seven ayahs. The sorting is trivially easy: three or four gems, well-spaced, deeply forgiving. The child is learning how to play, and learning that this game will never hurt them.

### World Two — The Orchard
*Al-Ma'un, Quraysh, Al-Fil, Al-Humazah, At-Takathur, Al-Qari'ah*

Afternoon, dappled shade, fruit heavy on branches. Levels grow slightly larger. Surahs reach eight to eleven ayahs. Gems are hidden a little better — behind foliage, above a climb. The sorting screen now holds enough gems that the child must genuinely recall the sequence, not just pattern-match by color.

### World Three — The Courtyard
*Al-Zalzalah, Al-Adiyat, Al-Bayyinah, Al-Qadr, Al-Alaq*

Late afternoon, warm stone, long shadows, arched colonnades. Architecture becomes the level. Verticality enters — climbing, descending, water below. The child begins to encounter surahs they have not memorized before. This is the pivot: the game moves from *reinforcing* to *teaching*.

### World Four — The Night Sky
*At-Tin, Ash-Sharh, Ad-Duha, Al-Layl, Ash-Shams*

Dusk into stars. The palette cools — deep pines, blues, silver. Levels are contemplative and open, more wandering than platforming. Surahs are longer and denser. The pace slows deliberately. By now, the child has played for weeks.

### Beyond
The structure extends as far as the child does. Juz Amma is the natural horizon of the first full release. What lies past it can wait.

## Small Things That Matter

**A surah, once completed, is never gone.** The world map keeps every level open forever. A child can return to Al-Ikhlas on any day and simply run through it to hear it again. Many will.

**The Recitation Room.** A quiet, non-game space reachable from the map. Every gem the child has ever collected, arranged on shelves by surah, glowing softly. Tap one, hear it. Tap a whole surah, hear it complete. No objective. Some children will spend more time here than in the levels, and that is entirely the point.

**One voice.** A single reciter across the whole game, chosen with care and consulted with properly. The child should come to know that voice the way they know a bedtime storyteller's.

**No score, no stars, no leaderboard.** Nothing that turns the Qur'an into a thing to be beaten. The only reward for finishing a surah is hearing it whole.

**The parent's page.** Tucked behind a simple gate. Which surahs the child has completed, how many times they've replayed each, which ayahs they consistently misorder. Quiet, useful, unobtrusive. A parent should be able to sit down at bedtime and know exactly what to ask their child to recite.

---

## Platform

Web, built to feel native on iPad. Saved to the home screen, it opens full-screen with its own icon and works offline. This means no App Store, no download, no cost of entry — a link, shared between parents, that simply works.

Landscape only. Two enormous soft-edged buttons for left and right; the entire right half of the screen jumps. Nothing requires precision. Nothing requires two fingers at once.

---

## What This Is Not

It is not a quiz. It is not a test. It is not a leaderboard, a streak, a daily login reward, or a set of achievements. It does not push notifications at a child. It does not have a shop.

It is a garden with gems in it, and a voice that reads.

---

## The Measure of Success

Not downloads. Not session length.

A six-year-old, in the back of a car, absently reciting a surah they never sat down to learn.
