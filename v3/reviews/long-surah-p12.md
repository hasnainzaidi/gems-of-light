# Long-surah Prototype 12 — Linked Stars

Played on: (tbd) · child: (tbd) · sessions: (tbd)

## The assertion

The ordinary stanza shrine makes Al-Lail's 21 ayat manageable by presenting
only `[4, 7, 5, 5]` at a time. That protects working memory, but it can hide
the most fragile part of chunked memorization: knowing how one stanza leads
into the next.

**Linked Stars asserts that each stanza after the first should begin with the
last ayah of the previous stanza.** The shrine therefore rehearses these
overlapping sets:

- 1–4
- 4–11
- 11–16
- 16–21

The overlap is deliberately small. It adds three placements to the full run,
but directly practices all three stanza seams without introducing text, a
quiz, a wrong-way branch, or a second gameplay loop.

## Child flow

The adventure and campfire are the familiar Al-Lail climb. At the shrine, the
first four gems are recalled normally and merge into a crest star. When the
next group arrives, the star gives back its final gem: ayah 4 appears again at
the front of the vv5–11 group. Placing 4 and then 5 makes the transition an
action the child performs. The same handoff repeats for 11→12 and 16→17.

Nothing explains the overlap. The returning gem and crest-star movement must
make it feel like a thread being carried forward, not like the game forgot a
gem that was already placed. A not-yet-correct choice still drifts home
quietly, and the existing listening and help behavior remains unchanged.

## Learning value and cost

The hoped-for gain is **associative chaining across stanza boundaries**. The
previous stanza's final ayah becomes the retrieval cue for the next stanza's
opening, while each active set stays phone-legible at five to eight gems.

The direct cost is repetition and fatigue. Al-Lail's shrine grows from 21 to
24 placements, and the largest set grows from seven to eight. A child may
experience the returning boundary gem as meaningful reinforcement, harmless
familiarity, or tedious duplication. If the child says or signals “I did this
already,” rushes through the repeated gem, or disengages late in the shrine,
the bridge is charging more attention than it earns.

## Playtest criteria

Test P12 independently, then compare it with the ordinary `[4, 7, 5, 5]`
stanza version. Focus observation on the three seams rather than only on Grand
Gem completion.

### Watch during play

- Does the child understand why ayah 4, 11, or 16 returns, without an adult
  explaining it?
- At each new stanza, do they place the repeated boundary gem promptly and
  then choose the following gem with fewer listens or misses?
- Does the crest-star-to-returning-gem animation read as “carry this forward”?
- Does the eight-gem second set remain visually clear and physically easy to
  use on a landscape phone?
- When do fidgeting, rapid guessing, or requests to stop begin relative to the
  baseline shrine?

### Record from telemetry

- First-try placement, misses, listens, and hints for ayat 4→5, 11→12, and
  16→17, separated from the rest of each stanza.
- Total shrine duration and time spent on each repeated boundary gem.
- Whether the child finishes the shrine and voluntarily replays or returns.
- Any late-run fatigue signal: faster random placements, rising listens per
  gem, or hints concentrated in the final stanza.

### Decision rule

Keep the bridge idea only if at least two of the three **next-stanza opening
ayat** (5, 12, 17) show clearer independent recall than the baseline, while
the child still completes the shrine without a visible fatigue increase. If
the returning gems confuse the child, the eight-gem fan becomes crowded, or
total engagement falls, reject P12 even if aggregate shrine accuracy rises.

The strongest partial result would justify keeping bridges only at transitions
that playtests identify as genuinely fragile, rather than automatically adding
all three.
