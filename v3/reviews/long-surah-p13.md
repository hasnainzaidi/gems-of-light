# Long-surah Prototype 13 — Four Lanterns · Design Review

Status: ready for child playtest. This document states the hypothesis and the
evidence needed for a verdict; it does not claim an unrun playtest result.

## The assertion

A long surah may need a short final payoff more than it needs exhaustive recall
in the same sitting. After the child has collected all 21 ayah gems in order
and heard Al-Lail straight through at the earned campfire, the shrine presents
only four shuffled gems: ayat 1, 5, 12, and 17, the openings of the four
thematic stanzas. Placing those four in order tests the surah's large-scale
shape and reaches the Grand Gem quickly.

This is deliberately **not detailed ayah retrieval**. Success can show that the
child recognizes the four major movements in order; it cannot show that they
know the order inside vv1–4, vv5–11, vv12–16, or vv17–21. That tradeoff is the
point of the prototype, not a hidden limitation.

## Child flow

1. Climb Al-Lail's existing night mountain and collect all 21 gems in order.
   Each collection keeps the current quiet listening pause, and the four rest
   ledges continue to give the platforming natural breaths.
2. Reach the summit campfire and hear the full surah once, uninterrupted.
3. Enter the shrine. Four gems float in a shuffled fan. Tapping or lifting one
   plays its ayah; one breathing socket asks what comes next.
4. A right gem settles and lights the next socket. A not-yet-right gem drifts
   home without blame; the existing shimmer and automatic help ensure that the
   ceremony always completes.
5. Four placements bloom the Wise Tree and form the Grand Gem.

The child sees no numbers, labels, score, failure state, or instructional text.

## Why it may help engagement

- The ending asks for four meaningful choices instead of 21 placements, so the
  reward arrives while the summit still feels exciting.
- The four openings act like lanterns at the entrances to four remembered
  stretches. They may give the child a usable mental map of a long recitation.
- The adventure remains substantial; this prototype changes the cognitive and
  interaction weight of the payoff rather than shortening the surah itself.

## Risks and honest limits

- Four correct placements can flatter knowledge. A child may know the openings
  while having little recall of the verses between them.
- Recognition by listening to every gem can solve the shrine without unaided
  macro-sequence recall. Listens per socket matter as much as completion.
- The 21 collection pauses plus full campfire recitation may already exhaust a
  child; shortening only the shrine may be too late to fix the pacing problem.
- The final shrine may feel slight after such a large climb, or the four selected
  gems may feel arbitrary if the stanza boundaries are not yet meaningful to
  the child.
- This should not replace the Remembering or another later opportunity for
  detailed, spaced retrieval. It tests a first-session ending, not a complete
  memorization strategy.

## Child playtest criteria

Compare P13 with the stanza-recall control under the same child, reciter, and
roughly similar time of day.

- **Completion:** Does the child reach the Grand Gem without asking to stop or
  disengaging during the shrine?
- **Macro recall:** Of the four sockets, how many first choices are correct
  before listening to a candidate gem? Record first-try, misses, listens, and
  hints; do not infer knowledge from completion alone.
- **Ending feel:** After the fourth placement, does the child seem satisfied,
  surprised that it ended, or eager for more? Ask only after play, in the
  child's own words, rather than explaining the experiment beforehand.
- **Pacing location:** Note the first visible fidget or request to quit. If it
  occurs during the climb or campfire, the four-gem shrine did not solve the
  main long-surah problem.
- **Next-day evidence:** In a later remembering session, can the child identify
  the four stanza openings in order with fewer listens? Also sample a few
  within-stanza transitions separately; macro improvement with weak internal
  order confirms the expected tradeoff rather than a full-memory win.
- **Replay desire:** Does the child voluntarily replay P13 more often than the
  control? A quicker ceremony is valuable only if the long climb still invites
  return.

## Decision rule

Keep this direction if it materially reduces shrine disengagement, preserves a
satisfying ending, and produces evidence of next-day macro sequence knowledge.
Combine it with a separate spaced detailed-recall path if internal stanza order
remains weak. Reject it if fatigue consistently arrives before the shrine, if
the four openings do not read as a coherent whole, or if the ending feels
unearned.
