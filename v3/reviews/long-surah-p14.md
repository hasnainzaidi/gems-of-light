# Long-surah Prototype 14 — Night-camp Shrines

Played on: (tbd) · child: (tbd) · sessions: (tbd)

## The assertion

P14 asserts that a 21-ayah surah should not save all of its recall work for one
long shrine at the end. Recall becomes part of the climb: each thematic stretch
ends at a small shrine, and completing that shrine permanently lights the camp
before the next stretch opens.

The four recall sets are deliberately overlapping:

- collect 1–4 → small shrine 1–4
- collect 5–11 → small shrine 4–11
- collect 12–16 → small shrine 11–16
- collect 17–21 → summit full-surah recitation → final small shrine 16–21
  → Grand Gem

The repeated ayat 4, 11, and 16 carry the final sound of one movement into the
opening of the next. P14 never follows the summit with a second 21-item shrine.
The last six-item shrine is the final recall task and earns the normal Grand
Gem.

## Child-facing flow

The child climbs Al-Lail exactly as before, collecting the awake gems in order
and pausing to hear each ayah. After ayah 4, the first small stone gate opens
and breathes with warm light. Walking through it opens a small shrine containing
only gems 1–4. When those four are rebuilt, the child returns to that same
ledge. Its gate remains physically open with a constellation shining above it,
the lower stretch remains restored, and ayah 5 is the next awake gem above.

The pattern repeats at the next two night camps. The second shrine carries ayah
4 forward before 5–11; the third carries ayah 11 before 12–16. Each completed
camp becomes a visible light on the mountain, so progress is expressed through
the world rather than text, numbers, or a chapter picker.

After the third camp, the child climbs the final stretch and collects 17–21.
The summit campfire then plays Al-Lail once, whole and uninterrupted, so the
four movements are heard as one surah. The door opens into the last compact
shrine, 16–21. Completing it blooms the Wise Tree and forms the Grand Gem.

There are no wrong paths, penalties, instructions, or quiz screens. A
not-yet-correct gem still drifts home quietly and the existing listening and
help behavior remains available.

## Checkpoints and returning

A checkpoint is earned only by completing its small shrine. Merely reaching a
ledge, collecting some gems beyond it, or leaving during a shrine does not mark
that movement learned.

On return, P14 restores every ayah through the latest completed camp, places
the child at that camp's ledge, keeps its constellation gate open, and wakes
the next unlearned ayah. The child does not have to reclimb or recollect a completed movement.
Partial progress after the last completed camp is intentionally discarded; the
previous lit camp is the safe place to resume.

Completing a camp shrine immediately returns the child to the climb, so all
four movements can still be played in one sitting. The checkpoints also create
honest stopping places if attention runs out: closing after a lit camp and
returning later should feel like continuing the same ascent, not restarting a
level.

## What this prototype may improve

- **Child-sized recall:** no active shrine set exceeds eight gems, and the
  final task is six rather than twenty-one.
- **Retrieval near learning:** each movement is reconstructed before many more
  ayat compete for attention.
- **Transition rehearsal:** the overlapping boundary gem directly joins
  4→5, 11→12, and 16→17.
- **Session flexibility:** a child may finish one meaningful stretch or keep
  climbing without losing completed learning.
- **A lighter ending:** the whole-surah campfire integrates the sound, then one
  final small recall produces the Grand Gem without a long duplicate shrine.

## Risks to watch closely

### Four gates may break the adventure

Three ledge shrines plus the final shrine may feel like repeated tollbooths.
Scene changes, shuffled gems, and return transitions can drain the momentum of
an otherwise satisfying climb. A child who repeatedly tries to walk past the
ready star, asks why play stopped, or becomes less eager after each gate is
telling us the gates cost too much.

### Immediate-memory inflation

Each small shrine arrives just after the child collected and heard that
movement. Strong first-try results may measure a fresh sensory trace rather
than durable sequence knowledge. An easy shrine in the same sitting is not, by
itself, evidence that the child will remember the chunk tomorrow. The most
useful comparison is a resumed or next-day run, not the first checkpoint run.

### Chunk integration remains incomplete

The overlap rehearses each seam and the summit campfire supplies one complete
listening, but the child never actively reconstructs all 21 ayat in one task.
They may learn four islands plus three bridges without gaining a stable sense
of the full arc. If transitions improve while later whole-surah recall does
not, P14 needs an integration idea elsewhere; restoring the exhausting final
21-item shrine is not automatically the answer.

### Checkpoint loss can feel unfair

Saving only completed shrines is pedagogically honest, but a child who exits
after collecting several gems in the next stretch may feel that the game erased
their work. Watch whether the lit camp feels like a safe home or an arbitrary
rollback.

## Morning playtest criteria

Start from a clean P14 lab save. Do not explain the stars or shrine schedule
unless the child is genuinely stuck; the prototype must teach its rhythm with
light and motion.

### During the climb

- Does the child notice and approach each breathing camp star independently?
- After shrine completion, do they understand that they returned to the same
  ledge and naturally continue upward?
- Does eagerness to continue hold across all four gates, or do hesitation,
  fidgeting, random placements, or requests to stop accumulate?
- Does the checkpoint rhythm make the mountain feel like four achievable
  victories, or four interruptions?
- At the summit, does the full recitation feel earned and integrating rather
  than like another delay?
- Is there visible relief or disappointment when the final shrine contains
  only 16–21 instead of the whole surah?

### At the transitions

For 4→5, 11→12, and 16→17, record:

- first-try placement of the repeated boundary ayah;
- listens and misses before the following new ayah is placed;
- hesitation between the boundary gem and the next gem;
- whether the returning gem reads as a helpful link or “I already did this.”

Transition results matter more than aggregate shrine accuracy. The bridge earns
its place only if it helps the child enter the next movement.

### Checkpoint test

After at least one completed camp, leave P14 and re-enter it later. Verify and
observe that:

- the child starts at the latest lit camp;
- completed gems and environmental restoration remain present;
- the next unlearned ayah, not ayah 1, is awake;
- the child recognizes this as continuation without adult explanation;
- recall after the delay remains reasonably calm, even if it is less perfect
  than the immediate shrine.

Also note what happens if play ends after collecting part of the next stretch:
returning to the previous completed camp is the intended behavior, but the
child's emotional response decides whether that rule is acceptable.

### Record from telemetry

- completion time, first-try placements, misses, listens, and hints for each
  of the four small shrines;
- time from returning to the ledge until movement resumes;
- abandonment point relative to each gate;
- same-sitting continuation versus later return after each lit camp;
- change in tries/listens per gem from immediate checkpoints to delayed play;
- total time to Grand Gem and whether the child voluntarily returns or
  replays.

## Decision rule

Keep P14's structure if children understand the lit-camp resume language,
complete more of Al-Lail without late-session fatigue, and show steadier recall
at at least two of the three stanza transitions. The final compact shrine
should feel sufficient after the whole-surah campfire, not anticlimactic.

Reject or simplify it if the gates repeatedly break play, immediate shrine
scores collapse after a delay, checkpoint rollback upsets the child, or the
four movements never cohere into one remembered surah. A mixed result may
support fewer gates, selectively placed only at the hardest transitions.
