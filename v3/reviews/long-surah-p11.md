# Prototype 11 — The Whole Night · Long-Surah Control

Prototype 11 deliberately preserves the current Al-Lail experience: one
continuous 21-gem mountain ascent, the full surah at the summit campfire, and
the existing stanza shrine in four movements of 4, 7, 5, and 5 ayat. Its save
is isolated from the real world and from the other long-surah experiments.

## Control hypothesis

A single uninterrupted adventure may give a child the strongest feeling of
undertaking and completing one whole surah. The long climb supplies a coherent
spatial sequence, while chunking only the shrine may be enough to keep recall
manageable without breaking the adventure into lessons.

This control asserts that long-surah difficulty belongs primarily in recall,
not traversal. It should be preferred if children remain engaged through the
whole ascent and understand the four shrine movements without needing visible
chapters or resumable intermediate goals.

## Child flow

1. The child begins at the foot of the night mountain.
2. Twenty-one gems wake one at a time in surah order. Each collection pauses
   movement so its ayah can be heard.
3. The child experiences one continuous climb, with quiet rest ledges between
   the surah's four thematic stretches but no chapter ceremony or checkpoint.
4. At the summit, the child sits and hears the complete surah uninterrupted.
5. The shrine presents only one stanza's 4–7 gems at a time. Each completed
   stanza compresses into a crest star before the next appears.
6. Completing all four stanza recalls forms the Grand Gem.

## Risks

- Twenty-one collection-and-listen beats may make the ascent feel endless or
  stop-start for a 5–8-year-old.
- A child who leaves before the summit may not feel that they completed a
  meaningful unit, even if some collection progress is stored.
- The full campfire recitation followed by four recall rounds may exceed the
  child's comfortable session length.
- The quiet terrain ledges may not read as stanza boundaries, so the spatial
  chunking intended by the recipe may be invisible to the child.
- A large undertaking can produce a memorable triumph, but it can also reduce
  voluntary replay if the child anticipates the entire climb again.

## Observable playtest decision criteria

Keep the whole-night structure when the child:

- reaches the summit without asking how much remains, trying to leave, or
  showing sustained fidgeting during later ayah pauses;
- can follow the next gem and climb without grown-up explanation;
- remains attentive through the summit recitation and enters the shrine
  willingly;
- understands each crest-star transition and completes all four stanza rounds
  without the later rounds showing sharply more misses, listens, or hints;
- describes or treats the experience as one satisfying journey and chooses to
  replay it voluntarily.

Reject or borrow from a chaptered alternative when the child:

- disengages consistently before ayah 12 or before the summit;
- asks for a stopping point, assumes a rest ledge is the end, or is upset that
  leaving means returning to the bottom;
- rushes, taps through, or leaves during the full-surah campfire;
- begins the shrine already fatigued, with recall quality dropping stanza by
  stanza;
- completes once but avoids replay because the world feels too long.

Compare Prototype 11 with the other long-surah builds using time and exits by
stanza boundary, summit completion, campfire attention, shrine tries/listens/
hints per stanza, and voluntary return. The control wins only if its stronger
whole-journey feeling is visible without costing completion, recall quality,
or the child's desire to come back.
